EMG-Controlled 3D Printed Prosthetic Hand

Assistive Technology • 2024

Overview

This project aimed to create an affordable, functional prosthetic hand controlled by electromyography (EMG) sensors that detect muscle signals. By combining 3D printing technology with Arduino-based electronics, the design achieves professional-level functionality at a fraction of traditional prosthetic costs.

Total Cost: Under $50

Compared to commercial prosthetics costing $10,000+, this design proves that advanced assistive technology can be accessible to everyone.

How It Works

The prosthetic hand uses EMG sensors placed on the user's residual limb to detect muscle contractions. These electrical signals are processed by an Arduino microcontroller, which translates them into finger movements through servo motors.

Signal Processing Pipeline

  • Signal Acquisition: EMG sensors detect electrical activity from muscle contractions
  • Amplification & Filtering: Raw signals are amplified and noise is filtered out
  • Pattern Recognition: Arduino identifies different muscle activation patterns
  • Motor Control: Servo motors actuate fingers based on recognized patterns
  • Feedback Loop: Visual and mechanical feedback helps users refine control

Design & Construction

Mechanical Design

  • 3D printed components using PLA for lightweight, durable structure
  • Finger joints designed with cable-driven actuation system
  • Palm structure sized for average adult proportions
  • Modular design allowing component replacement and upgrades
  • Cable routing system mimicking tendon structure of human hand

Electronic Components

  • Arduino Nano: Compact microcontroller for signal processing
  • MyoWare EMG Sensors: Medical-grade muscle signal detection
  • Servo Motors: Precise finger actuation with position feedback
  • Battery Pack: Rechargeable power supply for portable operation
  • Custom PCB: Integrated circuit board for clean wiring

Software & Control

  • Custom Arduino code for real-time signal processing
  • Threshold-based gesture recognition algorithm
  • Calibration routine adapting to individual muscle signatures
  • Multiple grip patterns (pinch, power grip, precision grip)
  • Safety features preventing over-torque on fingers

Technical Challenges Overcome

  • Signal Noise: Implemented hardware filtering and software algorithms to isolate muscle signals from electromagnetic interference
  • Power Efficiency: Optimized servo control to extend battery life while maintaining responsive control
  • Mechanical Strength: Reinforced stress points in 3D printed structure without significantly increasing weight
  • User Calibration: Developed adaptive threshold system accounting for varying muscle strength and signal amplitude
  • Cable Tension: Designed tensioning system maintaining proper finger position without binding

Results & Impact

The completed prosthetic hand successfully demonstrates that advanced assistive technology doesn't require expensive commercial solutions. Key achievements include:

  • Functional grip strength sufficient for everyday objects (books, cups, utensils)
  • Response time under 200ms from muscle signal to finger movement
  • Battery life exceeding 6 hours of continuous use
  • Total build cost of $48 (components + printing material)
  • Open-source design allowing others to replicate and improve

Skills Developed

  • Arduino programming and embedded systems development
  • 3D CAD modeling optimized for additive manufacturing
  • Cable-driven mechanism design
  • User-centered design for assistive technology
  • Iterative prototyping and testing methodologies
  • Circuit design and PCB layout

Future Improvements

Potential enhancements for future iterations:

  • Machine learning for improved gesture recognition
  • Haptic feedback providing sense of touch
  • Individual finger control for more dexterous manipulation
  • Smartphone app for configuration and calibration
  • Cosmetic covers matching skin tones
